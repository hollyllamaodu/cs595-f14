		<h1 itemprop="headline">Four Career Mistakes You Should Be Making -- And The One You Shouldn't</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

	<div class="comment_links">
		<a href="#comment_reply" class="scroll_to post_your_comment">Comment Now</a>

    <div class="followable_block initializing" data-target-type="article" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/2868-1509" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Four Career Mistakes You Should Be Making -- And The One You Shouldn't&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-type-advoice="false" data-target-name="" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow">

					<div class="body_inner">



						
<p><em>“We create as much information every two days as we have since the dawn of civilization through the year 2003.” – Don Peppers (Peppers &amp; Rogers Group)</em></p> 
<p>Consider that statistic. That statistic was posted near the end of last year.</p> 
<p>Enormous amounts of information is generated every single second. There are more blogs, more news stories, more articles, more Tweets, more videos, books, and the list goes on. Instantly, any one of us can find a “How-To, Where-To, and What-To,” on any given subject. And most of the information is up to date—almost in real-time.&nbsp; Every day you can find new insights, ponderings, advice, ratings, rankings, and comments. But how do you filter and leverage it so it actually benefits you and your career or business?</p> 
<p>The two of us have spent most of our careers focused on figuring out what actions and activities improve business, relationships, innovation, and outcomes. And, although we’ve had the opportunity to curiously examine data collected in some of the largest workplace studies ever, like the <a title="Great Work Study" href="http://www.octanner.com/institute/white-papers/the-great-work-study" target="_blank">Great Work Study</a>, which analyzed 1.7 million cases of award-winning work, there was something else that caught our attention that we hadn’t taken a closer look at—mistakes.</p> 
<p>In a recent <a title="Forbes Interview" href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/davidsturt/2014/07/29/3-promises-to-drive-extreme-growth-ceo-coach-sheldon-harris-spills-the-beans/" target="_blank">Forbes Interview</a>, former CEO of Cold Stone Creamery, Sheldon Harris shared with us one of his mistakes early in his career that forever changed the way he leads people. He formally reprimanded an employee for repeatedly coming in late, only to discover that he had been voluntarily cleaning up trash in the parking lot every morning before coming inside. It’s a classic tale of “foot in mouth” syndrome.</p> 
<p>Or, consider what it feels like to watch $125 million literally disintegrate. This is exactly what happened when some NASA scientists made miscalculations due to the use of English units instead of metric units on the <a href="/companies/mars/">Mars</a> <span class="quotecard_hook" data-ticker="null" data-exchange="null" data-type="organization" data-naturalid="fred/company/13864" data-quotes-closing="0.0" data-quotes-now="0.0"><span class="wrapper"><a href="/companies/mars/">Mars</a></span></span> Climate Orbiter. The spacecraft disintegrated as it passed through the upper atmosphere.</p> 
<div style="padding: 20px 0pt; margin: 20px 0pt; border-bottom: 1px solid #DDDDDD; border-top: 1px solid #DDDDDD;">
 <a style="text-decoration: none; color: #900; font: bold 20px/26px Georgia;" href="http://www.forbes.com/ebooks/find-and-keep-your-dream-job-the-definitive-careers-guide-from-forbes/?utm_source=articlelink&amp;utm_medium=direct&amp;utm_campaign=careerebook">The Forbes eBook: Find And Keep Your Dream Job</a>
 <br /> 
 <a style="text-decoration: none; font: 15px/18px  Georgia; color: #000;" href="http://www.forbes.com/ebooks/find-and-keep-your-dream-job-the-definitive-careers-guide-from-forbes/?utm_source=articlelink&amp;utm_medium=direct&amp;utm_campaign=careerebook"><em>The Definitive Careers Guide From Forbes</em> encompasses every aspect of the job hunt, from interview to promotion. Written by some of Forbes’ best careers and leadership writers, it is available now for download.</a>
</div> 
<p>But, not all mistakes are so cut and dry. On a recent phone call, media and real estate mogul (and former Celebrity Apprentice Star) <a title="Nely Galan" href="http://www.nelygalan.com/" target="_blank">Nely Galan</a> shared with us a story about her teenage years where everyone thought she was making a mistake. It involved a headstrong battle with the Nuns who ran her Catholic School. However, the incident served as a major stepping-stone for who Nely has become today. Nely shared the story on <a title="The Big Idea" href="http://www.nelygalan.com/videos/the-big-idea-nely-reveals-her-millionaire-mindset/" target="_blank">The Big Idea</a> with Donny Deutsh.</p> 
<p>Consider these ‘mistakes’ for a second. They made us pause. And, they made us curious to find out how certain mistakes can actually be more valuable teachers than successes. Are there some mistakes you should make and some you shouldn’t?</p> 
<p>Looking to find answers to the question, ‘What are the valuable mistakes everyone should make in their career?’ we asked people we knew: “What mistakes have you made in your career that benefitted you in the long term?” and “What mistakes have you made in your career that hurt you in the long term?”</p> 
<div id="attachment_1512" class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 1660px">
 <img class=" wp-image-1512" alt="Forbes2" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/davidsturt/files/2014/09/Forbes2.png" />
 <p class="wp-caption-text">Image provided by Shutterstock</p>
</div> 
<p><strong>The answers we received about mistakes that benefitted respondents fell into the following four categories.</strong></p> 
<p><b>1. Say “Yes” to everything.</b> It’s a common mistake, especially early in a career.&nbsp; Saying ‘Yes’ can stretch a person or team thin. It can lead you into unchartered territories and, it can actually lead you to failure. Nevertheless, many of the people we talked to said they learned a lot from saying ‘yes’. One respondent said they learned ‘how to draw a line in the sand, and where to draw it.’ But, the answer that gave us the biggest ah-ha was from Dr. Murthy Renduchintala, Co-President of QCT at <a href="/companies/qualcomm/">Qualcomm</a> <span class="quotecard_hook" data-ticker="QCOM" data-exchange="NASDAQ" data-type="organization" data-naturalid="fred/company/3523" data-quotes-closing="73.65" data-quotes-now="74.86"><span class="wrapper"><a href="/companies/qualcomm/">Qualcomm</a></span></span> in San Diego, California. “This company started with seven people. The founders had a simple vision to improve communications—and that meant saying ‘Yes’ to possibility. We were the company that, no matter what the problem, or the deadline, or the situation, would find a solution.”</p> 
<p><b>2. Work for the jerk. </b>Bad bosses are great filters for showing us both where we don’t want to work, and how we don’t want to treat people. As one respondent named Christa noted, ‘It seemed like a mistake at the time to accept the position because I had heard the manager was horrible. But, I really wanted to gain the experience. And, I learned a lot more about myself than I ever expected.’</p> 
<p><b>3. Get fired for what you believe. </b>Not all of us will get tested in this area. This advice came from an employee who refused to read a specific script to some vendors because the employee felt the script was misleading and ethically irresponsible. That employee was let go. And, they were happy to leave.&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;<b></b></p> 
<p><b>4. Assume you’re always right.&nbsp; </b>Of course we all want to appear to our coworkers and customers that we know everything about our company, service, or product. But, the truth is, none of us know it all and more than a few of the people we spoke to, told us how valuable it was to have their ego crushed. Why is this a great mistake to make? Because, once you know you can be wrong, you approach work differently knowing that your next decision might not be correct, but it will be a learning experience that leads you closer and closer to finding the most successful result.<b></b><br /> 
 <!--nextpage--><br /> <b>&nbsp;</b><b>The fifth mistake: the one you don’t want to make.</b></p> 
<p><b>&nbsp;</b>It was difficult for us to create a label for the one mistake people said they wish they had never made. “Allowing some of the best people to leave” was one response. “Not realizing that I was mistreating people,” was another.&nbsp; However, if we had to create a nutshell to summarize the answers we received when we asked the question “What mistakes have you made in your career that hurt you in the long term?” the answers focused on one simple thing. They had nothing to do with productivity. They had nothing to do with performance. They focused on the failure to appreciate a relationship. So, here’s our attempt to categorize the one mistake you shouldn’t make…</p> 
<p><strong>5. Not saying “Thank You”</strong> None of us would be who we are today without the people who work with us, for us, and in spite of us. Maybe it’s time to show those people some gratitude…so all our mistakes can be learning experiences instead of regrets. </p>
<p class="sigfile"><em><span lang="EN" style="color: black; line-height: 150%; font-family: 'Calibri','sans-serif'; font-size: 12pt; mso-ascii-theme-font: major-latin; mso-hansi-theme-font: major-latin; mso-bidi-font-family: Helvetica; mso-themecolor: text1; mso-ansi-language: EN;">Learn more about the NYT Bestselling book <b><a href="http://www.amazon.com/Great-Work-Make-Difference-People/dp/0071818359/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1384292231&amp;sr=1-1&amp;keywords=great+work+how+to+make+a+difference+people+lovehttp://"><span style="color: black; mso-themecolor: text1;">Great Work: How to Make a Difference People Love</span></a>.</b></span></em></p>
