		<h1 itemprop="headline">If You Think Leadership Development Is A Waste Of Time You May Be Right</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->
		<div class="bylines">

        <cite class="entity_block type_inline_block">
        	
            <span class="attach_user_popup" data-user-json="" data-user-id="">

				<div class="body">

					<div class="body_inner">



						
<p><span style="color: #000000">There’s a dirty little secret in <a href="http://www.forbes.com/leadership/">leadership</a> development circles: many of us in the industry wonder at times how effective our work actually is. Sure, we can develop a rousing program, present compelling concepts, and entertain the crowd. That’s often what clients ask of us. But there are a myriad of factors that go into producing better leaders. How does spending hours cooped up in a contained learning environment actually end up producing more effective leaders? It’s a question worth exploring.</span></p> 
<p>A <a href="http://www.bersin.com/Store/details.aspx?docid=15587"><span style="text-decoration: underline">2012 study</span></a> found that American companies spend almost $14 billion annually on leadership development training. <span style="color: #000000">There are thousands and thousands of books on leadership (including mine), training targeted at any function or level, and executive programs at the world’s most esteemed universities. </span>In other words, there’s a lot of energy and resources being spent on these programs. This is a good thing. We know we need skillful leaders, and that leadership can be learned.</p> 
<p>However, so many times, after the leadership program ends, new behaviors aren’t sustained. <a href="http://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ210310"><span style="text-decoration: underline"><span style="color: #0563c1">Studies</span></span></a> have found that adult learners in a lecture setting forget nearly 50% of what they learn within two weeks. &nbsp;And consider that the most highly trained leaders – CEOs – are often not able to translate their knowledge into experience. The <a href="http://www.ccl.org/leadership/pdf/research/ExecutiveIntegration.pdf"><span style="text-decoration: underline">Center for Creative Leadership</span></a> found that 38% of new chief executives fail in their first 18 months on the job.</p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">So what factors make for a rich, enduring leadership development program? What factors actually create better leaders? What makes the investment in leadership training worth it?</span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Here are several considerations. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000"><strong>#1: Context. </strong></span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">We know from </span><a href="http://www.amazon.com/Moonwalking-Einstein-Science-Remembering-Everything/dp/0143120530"><span style="text-decoration: underline"><span style="color: #0563c1">research</span></span></a><span style="color: #000000"> that content that’s relevant to one’s own experience and builds upon prior knowledge is most likely to be retained. Leadership programs must integrate with the larger scope of the organization. Bringing in someone to deliver the standard pump-up-the-troops program may be briefly engaging, but not produce any behavior change.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Before spending a dime on training, it’s important to clarify: what is the purpose and goal? It’s far more effective to focus on specific leadership skills that are pertinent to the vision and growth of the company and make sure the design is carefully tailored to them.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">In an article titled “</span><a href="http://www.mckinsey.com/insights/leading_in_the_21st_century/why_leadership-development_programs_fail"><span style="text-decoration: underline">Why Leadership Development Programs Fail”</span></a><span style="color: #000000"> put out by <a href="/companies/mckinsey-company/">McKinsey &amp; Company</a> <span class="quotecard_hook" data-ticker="null" data-exchange="null" data-type="organization" data-naturalid="fred/company/13089" data-quotes-closing="0.0" data-quotes-now="0.0"><span class="wrapper"><a href="/companies/mckinsey-company/">McKinsey &amp; Company</a></span></span>, the authors discuss the problems–and solution–to these general approaches to leadership development: </span>“What we often find is a long list of leadership standards, a complex web of dozens of competencies, and corporate-values statements. Each is usually summarized in a seemingly easy-to-remember way (such as the three Rs), and each on its own terms makes sense. In practice, however, what managers and employees often see is an ‘alphabet soup’ of recommendations. We have found that when a company cuts through the noise to identify a small number of leadership capabilities essential for success in its business—such as high-quality decision making or stronger coaching skills—it achieves far better outcomes.”</p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000"><strong>#2: Continual motion.</strong> </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">A&nbsp;training program should be viewed as the beginning of the leadership development process, not the end. It’s critical that after the program is complete, there’s a sustained effort to maintain momentum and individualize learning. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">There are many ways to accomplish this. Some companies include personal executive coaching. Others establish a learning cohort that meets regularly and discusses concepts. It can also be valuable to conduct shorter trainings over a longer period of time, i.e. 4 one-day sessions rather than 1 four-day session. By stretching the learning out, you avoid overload and allow the concepts to be gradually advanced in a real-world context.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000"><strong>#3: Measurement. </strong></span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">This is one of the hardest ones – how do you actually measure something like leadership? Many well-meaning measurement protocols end up backfiring. Consider the number of participants promoted, which is often a measurement of program success. If the company has a few bad quarters and cuts back, then that metric becomes meaningless. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Most companies use what my friend calls smile sheets: those ubiquitous ratings of 1-5 on score sheets you fill out at the end of a program. They measure how much participants enjoyed the program, and while a consideration, is not a meaningful goal. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Companies need to carefully consider what measurement should be well ahead of time. Are people getting promoted faster? Are managers seeing an improvement on performance reviews? Is the pool of succession candidates getting deeper? It often takes a year or more to see this fleshed out so patience and clarity are both key.</span><br /> 
 <!--nextpage--><br /> <b><span style="color: #000000">#4: Courageous content.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></b></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Peter Bregman, business advisor and author of </span><a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0446583405/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0446583405&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=petebreg-20"><i><span style="text-decoration: underline">18 Minutes: Find Your Focus, Master Distraction, and Get the Right Things Done</span></i></a>, argues that leadership development programs strive too earnestly to create safe environments for learning. But the best learning often comes in times of risk and struggle.</p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Leadership programs need to place people in real life-situations, not just provide case studies. “If the challenge of leadership is emotional courage, then emotional courage is what we need to teach. You can’t just learn about communication, you have to do it, in the heat of the moment, when the pressure is on, and your emotions are high,” Bregman writes.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">For a leader to actually learn new skills, he or she has to be put in situations that test their capacities and knowledge in order to ultimately improve. As an example, I often do a real-life feedback exercise in my programs that initially causes discomfort and even pushback. But participants learn about their own hesitancies in the process and test out new strategies. In the end, it’s consistently rated as the best takeaway of the program. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000"><strong>#5:</strong> <strong>Clear and honest program rationale. </strong></span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Finally, we also have to be courageous about the reason for the leadership program in the first place. If there’s a serious deficit that needs to be addressed, it’s better to be honest about it. If high performers are being put into a program to escalate promotion, then be clear about that. If the program is meant to top-grade skills for current positions, that’s fine too. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000">Everyone is well meaning, and this can get in the way. Too often, companies disguise, or don’t know, the real reason for the program. They worry about setting incorrect expectations. Just because a senior leader wants it doesn’t make a program meaningful. Greater transparency about why participants are selected and what the program will lead to is critical. Otherwise, there’s widespread skepticism and even frustration which foils learning every time. </span></p> 
<p><span style="color: #000000"><span style="font-family: Times New Roman"><em>Kristi Hedges is a leadership coach, speaker and author of </em></span></span><a title="Power of Presence" href="http://www.amazon.com/Power-Presence-Unlock-Potential-Influence/dp/0814417736/ref=sr_1_4?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1314901156&amp;sr=8-4" target="_blank"><span style="text-decoration: underline"><span style="color: #0563c1">Power of Presence: Unlock Your Potential to Influence and Engage Others</span></span></a><span style="color: #000000"><strong>. </strong><i>She blogs at</i> </span><a href="http://www.kristihedges.com/"><span style="text-decoration: underline"><span style="color: #0563c1">kristihedges.com</span></span></a><span style="text-decoration: underline"><span style="color: #0563c1">.</span></span></p>

<p>As you say, Kristi, leadership development has failed in spite of $14 billion spent on it last year and thousands of books. Giving more guidelines is not the solution. What we need to know is why we have failed before we can determine a fix.</p> 
<p>Having had to first get rid of conventional leadership advice and then start figuring out what employees needed in order to excel (their performance eventually rose to a level 4 times higher than I had thought humanly possible), I have an answer. I note here that Richard Branson, certainly one of the most if not the most successful entrepreneur today states that he has never read any management or leadership books (smart).</p> 
<p>The error is that the leadership industry focuses on studying leaders. They don’t study followers or what followers follow. This science tells us exactly what leadership is (it is not what the books say it is) and what actions will and which will not lead people to be highly motivated, highly committed, and fully engaged Superstars who love to come to work. Unfortunately, managers act the wrong way far more often than they act the right way. The elephant in the room is the command and control approach to managing people. People cannot be directed to be highly motivate or have high morale. But they can be led to be highly motivated and have high morale.</p>
