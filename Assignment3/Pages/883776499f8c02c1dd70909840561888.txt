		<a href="/forbesasia/" class="slug">Forbes Asia</a>

		<h1 itemprop="headline">Mobile Master: KakaoTalk Creator Becomes One Of South Korea's Richest Billionaires</h1>


		<div class="magazine_issue_date">
			This story appears in the October 20, 2014 issue of Forbes Asia.
		</div>

			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

	<div class="comment_links">
		<a href="#comment_reply" class="scroll_to post_your_comment">Comment Now</a>
		<div class="comments_follow followable_block">




    <div class="followable_block initializing" data-target-type="article" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/1904-8398" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Mobile Master: KakaoTalk Creator Becomes One Of South Korea's Richest Billionaires&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-type-advoice="false" data-target-name="" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow">

			<div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">

				<div class="body">

					<div class="body_inner">

							<figure class="featured_image">
								<img src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/ryanmac/files/2014/09/Brian2_Chairman-of-Kakao-e1411495505117-1940x1090.jpeg" alt="" itemprop="image" />
								<figcaption>Kakao Corp. Chairman and cofounder Brian Kim Bum-Soo is worth at least $3.4 billion ahead of his company's merger and public stock listing with South Korean internet company Daum. (Photo courtesy of Kakao Corp.)</figcaption>
							</figure>


						
<p>On KakaoTalk, South Korea’s number one mobile-messaging service, users spend hours crushing candy on popular games, sharing photos and videos with friends, and sending cute emoticons and digital stickers. That’s all thanks to Kakao Corp. cofounder and chairman, “Brian” <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/kim-bum-soo/">Kim Bum-Soo</a>, who launched the service in 2010 and ever since has been perfecting a mobile universe inhabited by 93% of domestic smartphone users–or nearly 75% of the country’s 50 million people. KakaoTalk has now made Kim a billionaire.</p> 
<p>Kakao is merging with Daum, the country’s second-largest Internet portal, and is listing on the South Korean Stock Exchange on Oct. 1. Kim, 48, will own 40% of the combined company, and analysts predict a minimum value of $8.5 billion. On that basis Kim will be worth at least $3.4 billion, putting him among the country’s ten richest people. In April, a month before the Kakao-Daum deal was announced, FORBES ASIA estimated his wealth at $960 million. “Korea is known as the ‘land of the morning calm,’ and I would call him the ‘morning calm billionaire,’ ” says Eric Kim, a managing partner at Goodwater Capital who sits on Kakao’s board. “ He is a thoughtful, brilliant entrepreneur who’s made it his personal mission to have Kakao thrive as a global communications platform.” (Through a company spokeswoman, Brian Kim declined to comment for this story.)</p> 
<p>While Kakao, in Pangyo–south of Seoul–has certainly aimed globally, its greatest success has come at home. It boasts 50 million users who are active monthly, of which three-quarters are Koreans who’ve ditched SMS text messages for the ease of KakaoTalk. More important, KakaoTalk has evolved into a platform where users can spend time gaming, shopping and social networking. That’s created a highly lucrative technology company in a country that is behind only the U.S. and Japan in total Android-app-store revenue, according to AppAnnie. Overall, Kakao has 158 million registered users, with many of them in Japan, Southeast Asia and the U.S.</p> 
<div id="attachment_8429" class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 660px">
 <img class="size-full wp-image-8429" alt="Easing the ride: Three-quarters of KakaoTalk’s 50 million active users are in South Korea. (credit:Tim Clayton/Corbis)" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/ryanmac/files/2014/09/0924_kakao_650x455.jpg" />
 <p class="wp-caption-text">Easing the ride: Three-quarters of KakaoTalk’s 50 million active users are in South Korea. (credit:Tim Clayton/Corbis)</p>
</div> 
<p>Kakao’s emergence as the dominant player in South Korea can be attributed to Kim’s prescient timing in starting his company, says Joseph Chung, an analyst at Woori Investment &amp; Securities. Smartphones arrived in the country around 2009, and Kim quickly seized the opportunity to develop a messenger that could replace clunky SMS texts. He launched KakaoTalk in March of the following year, undercutting U.S.-based WhatsApp by offering itself for free (as opposed to WhatsApp’a annual 99-cent fee) and catering directly to Koreans. Kim and his team were also fast in creating apps for Kakao, especially games that made users more dependent on the service and eventually allowed it to generate revenue. “People had doubts that [the mobile messenger] could be monetized even though it was a big traffic gainer,” says Woori’s Chung. “But Brian Kim had insight on how to monetize social games like those made famous in the U.S. with Zynga and <a href="http://www.forbes.com/facebook-ipo/">Facebook</a>.”</p> 
<p>Games such as Anipang, a social puzzle game, and Candy Crush, a Western diversion modified for the KakaoTalk platform, make up 64% of the company’s revenue, according to Chung. Kakao says its revenue hit $200 million last year, and profits came in at $52 million, making it one of just a few mobile-messaging companies that have turned a profit. That makes the company a hot property in light of high-profile acquisitions of moneylosing outfits–such as <a href="/companies/rakuten/">Rakuten</a> <span class="quotecard_hook" data-ticker="null" data-exchange="null" data-type="organization" data-naturalid="fred/company/3541" data-quotes-closing="0.0" data-quotes-now="0.0"><span class="wrapper"><a href="/companies/rakuten/">Rakuten</a></span></span> of Japan’s $900 million purchase of Viber this year–and investment rounds at astronomical valuations (see: Snapchat and Tango). Some analysts are valuing the new company, which is expected to be called Daum Kakao, at more than $10 billion. (Daum’s founder, Lee Jae-Woong, will have about a 3% stake.)</p> 
<p>A valuation that high would serve as validation for Kim, who’s ceded day-to-day control of Kakao to co-chief executives Lee Jae-Beom and Sirgoo Lee but remains chairman. The son of parents who didn’t make it past grade school, Kim grew up dirt poor in Dam-yang in a family of eight that shared one bedroom. He found his escape when he was accepted into Seoul National University; he paid his way through school by offering private tutoring, and he sometimes skipped meals to save money.</p> 
<p>After finishing with undergraduate and master’s degrees in industrial engineering, Kim worked at Samsung for six years, leaving in 1998 to found online gaming company Hangame. He merged that company with another Korean Internet outfit, Naver, in 2000, creating NHN, which would come to dominate the country’s search and Web portal traffic.</p> 
<p>Kim served as NHN’s CEO and in other leadership roles until his departure in 2007 after differences with <a href="/companies/naver/">Naver</a> <span class="quotecard_hook" data-ticker="null" data-exchange="null" data-type="organization" data-naturalid="fred/company/3071" data-quotes-closing="0.0" data-quotes-now="0.0"><span class="wrapper"><a href="/companies/naver/">Naver</a></span></span> founder <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/lee-hae-jin/">Lee Hae-Jin</a> over the direction of the business. After dabbling in a series of Web 2.0 experiments, Kim dove into Kakao after seeing the rising prominence of smartphone technology. “I once asked him, “How big do you want to make this?’ ” recalls Osuke Honda, a partner with venture capital firm DCM who invested in KakaoTalk’s first round of institutional funding in 2011. “ I asked him, ‘What if Facebook came and offered $1 billion?’ ‘My former company is worth $10 billion,’ he said. ‘I want to make Kakao bigger than NHN.’ ”</p> 
<p><img class=" size-full wp-image-8430" alt="0923_forbesasia-kakao-chart_800" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/ryanmac/files/2014/09/0923_forbesasia-kakao-chart_800.jpg" /></p> 
<p>Some of Kim’s biggest competition comes from his old company, which started its own mobile messenger, Line, a year after KakaoTalk began. Once derided by Kakao fans as a copycat, Line has come nowhere near dethroning Kakao in South Korea, but it has become the dominant messaging service in Japan and has made a wider global impact. It reached the 470 million registered-user mark this summer, and Lee’s wealth topped $1 billion this year. (Last year NHN split into NHN Entertainment and Naver Corp., and Line, a Naver unit, filed in July for an initial public offering with a $10 billion valuation.)</p> 
<p>KakaoTalk’s ability to spread to other markets is further confined by other mobile-messaging competitors, from WhatsApp to China-based Tencent’s WeChat, all elbowing each other to find the next emerging market. Thus far it hasn’t been successful in finding a new market where it can replicate South Korea’s fanaticism for its products. “Kakao is trying to expand its user base in Southeast Asia, but Line and WeChat are much, much stronger there,” says Woori analyst Chung, who sees the company’s growth opportunities in the addition of more features for current users. “It’s all domestic business [for Kakao].”</p> 
<p>Indeed, the company is churning out new products and ideas that will make KakaoTalk even more ubiquitous in the lives of its users. Last year the company launched music and style applications, with the latter featuring deals on clothing that users can take advantage of themselves or send as gifts for friends. Last month the company got into the mobile-payments game with KakaoPay, and an Uber-esque smartphone-linked&nbsp;taxi service may soon be on the way. For the “morning calm billionaire,” the mobile universe is ever expanding.</p>
