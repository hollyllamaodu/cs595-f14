		<a href="/technology" class="slug" itemprop="articleSection">Tech</a>

	<time itemprop="datePublished" content="2014-09-24T09:37:00-04:00">9/24/2014 @ 9:37AM</time>

		<h1 itemprop="headline">Flash Forward: Cheap Memory Allows Apple To Keep Margins High While Improving The iPhone</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

	<div class="comment_links">
		<a href="#comment_reply" class="scroll_to post_your_comment">Comment Now</a>
		<div class="comments_follow followable_block">




    <div class="followable_block initializing" data-target-type="article" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/2366-7528" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Flash Forward: Cheap Memory Allows Apple To Keep Margins High While Improving The iPhone&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-type-advoice="false" data-target-name="" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow">

			<div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">

				<div class="body">

					<div class="body_inner">

							<figure class="featured_image">
								<img src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/markrogowsky/files/2014/09/Screenshot-2014-09-24-06.30.09-e1411565478363.png" alt="" itemprop="image" />
								<figcaption>The insides of an iPhone 6 Plus (Image credit: iFixit)</figcaption>
							</figure>


						
<p>The wizards at IHS have <a href="http://press.ihs.com/press-release/design-supply-chain/iphone-6-plus-100-costlier-consumers-buy%E2%80%94just-1550-more-expensive-">torn apart the latest iPhone</a>&nbsp;and concluded that it cost Apple right around $200 to build. Given than an iPhone 6 carries a retail price before subsidy of $649, that means Apple will earn an astounding 69 percent on the phone before accounting for things like software, warranty and support costs. Perhaps more remarkable, though, is the near metronome-like consistency in that figure over the past several years. From the introduction of the iPhone 4s through the 6, Apple’s phones have come to market with a build cost of between $195 and $207 while gaining capabilities every year. That this year’s model is smack in the middle of that range speaks to both Apple’s ability to manage its supply chain and also the relentless price declines of some key iPhone components as time passes.</p> 
<p><strong>Then and now</strong></p> 
<p>The 4s, when it came to market 3 years ago in 2011, lacked many features of the current models. It didn’t have 4G LTE, for starters, being Apple’s last 3G-only model. But it also lacked the TouchID fingerprint sensor and had just a 3.5-inch touchscreen, smaller than the 4-inch one in the 5/5s and, of course, the 4.7-inch display in the iPhone 6. Still with a much less capable radio and much smaller display, the iPhone 4s cost Apple $196, <a href="https://technology.ihs.com/389429/iphone-4s-carries-bom-of-188-ihs-isuppli-teardown-analysis-reveals">IHS reported at the time</a>. Of that, Apple was spending $19.20 on flash memory to store your pictures, music and apps and $9.10 on RAM to allow you to run those apps.</p> 
<p>Move ahead to today and that $28.30 has been sliced to $15. With the extra $13, Apple has been able to spend $8 more on the display ($45 vs. $37) and more on the processor ($20 vs. $15). Those two components provide many of the performance gains that have occurred over the past 3 years, allowing apps to run faster and, obviously, allowing you a better view of what you’re doing. But today’s screens are also higher in quality, with better contrast and color.</p> 
<p>Still, Apple has done things like adding like the TouchID fingerprint sensor, which IHS estimated at upwards of $15. To find the budget for that, the company has managed to save significantly on the cost of cameras, even though they, too, have improved annually. The cost has fallen from $17.60 to $11. Smaller savings have been found in power management, WiFi and Bluetooth. Those have helped free up budget for things like the NFC chip that enables Apple Pay in the new phones.&nbsp;The sum total is that today’s iPhone is just $4 more costly but far more than $4 more capable.</p> 
<p>Interestingly, while Apple appears to buying screens from LG Display and Japan Display — cutting out Samsung — it seems certain that the A8 processor is still being made by both its rival as well as TSMC, with each supplying have IHS thinks. Samsung has traditionally been the sole manufacturer of Apple’s A-series processors. Another longtime partner, however, is still in favor as Corning’s Gorilla Glass is still protecting the screen after countless feints and rumors suggesting Apple was switching to sapphire proved either false or premature, depending on who you believe.</p> 
<p><strong>The pluses (without many minuses)</strong></p> 
<p>Some major changes should allow Apple to capture even higher margins this year than in the recent past. While the 5.5-inch iPhone 6 Plus is unlikely to sell in anywhere near the volumes of the 6, it will capture many more margin dollars for every unit that moves. IHS says the incremental build cost is just $15.50, with half of that coming from the big screen and a bit from the optical image stabilization in the camera. Apple charges $100 more, of course, for the larger model.</p> 
<p>That could mean that upselling to the Plus now eclipses what had been the most profitable part of the Apple empire: Upselling to larger amounts of storage on iPhones. In past years, Apple got an extra $100 to move you from 16GB to 32GB and then $100 more to go to 64GB. This year, the first $100 takes you all the way to 64GB.</p> 
<p>With IHS speculating that Apple is paying 42 cents per gigabyte, that makes the cost of the extra flash $20.16. And the 64 to 128 upgrade runs Apple $26.88. While the margins on those upgrades are still the kind of thing that most gadget makers would kill for, it’s a significant reduction from the recent past. With the iPhone 5s, for example, IHS believes Apple spent just $10-20 on the upgrade tiers while getting $100 more for them. Back in the 4s days, those figures were $20 and $40, respectively, which serves as a reminder of the march of technology’s progress. Apple makes more margin today offering you a boost from 64GB to 128GB for $100 than it did offering the upgrade from 32GB to 64GB back in 2011. Everybody’s a winner thanks to Moore’s Law.</p> 
<p>Early anecdotal reporting suggests that the change to the upgrade tiers is motivating more buyers to select the 64GB this year than had selected the 32GB models in the past. Whether that sticks past the early upgraders remains to be seen, but it could have positive implications for Apple in the form of <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/chuckjones/2014/09/19/can-apple-increase-the-iphones-asp-and-gross-margin-by-giving-away-memory/">higher average selling prices on iPhones and improved gross margins</a>. What’s especially noteworthy is that iPhone gross margins tend to be lower early in the new product cycle and improve late. If more buyers are selecting the Plus and larger memory configurations, Apple might surprise Wall Street there.</p> 
<p><strong>What next?</strong></p> 
<p>Less immediately clear is what happens with the older products Apple sells. Last year’s 5s, for example, was estimated at $195 and there are some obvious places where Apple is saving now on building it (memory, processor, camera). But those amount to perhaps a bit more than $10 overall. The phone price has obviously fallen by $100, however. Even if you account for non-obvious improvements in component costs, the way this is possible isn’t readily clear from simply looking at the bill of materials.</p> 
<p>And that leads us to an important caveat about IHS’ numbers: They don’t include a true accounting of the engineering, capital expenditures, et al. that goes into making an iPhone. Apple’s continued use of the chassis from the iPhone 5, for example, means that assembly partner Foxconn has now become quite adept at building that model and using the special equipment purpose built to put it together. The total accounting cost of building a 5s is higher than it might appear just looking at the IHS data, but the gap between one of those and the iPhone 6 is doubtless larger.</p>
