              Ranching and farming livelihoods are being thrown into instability, insecurity, and uncertainty.
        </h3>

    <div class="entry-content">
      The consequences of climate change are detailed in the news with an abundance of numbers concerning shortfalls in rain, extreme droughts, and record-high temperatures. But statistics and data fail to show the tax and torment that occurs for the farmers and ranchers like me whose livelihood is at stake.<br>
 <br>
         My world has been centered on that type of living since growing up on my family’s cattle ranch in northern New Mexico. That ranch helped shape the person I am today, a farmer and horse veterinarian in Mora County, New Mexico. The difference between now and then is the debilitating blow the new weather norms have had on our work. <br>
<br>
<img src="https://secure.assets.bostatic.com/apps/madison/media/blog_images/a025257d23eb49258df56379c4da7c16_DougNM_large.jpg"><span style="background-color: initial;">Intense heat and frequent droughts are the new challenges I have to face. The snowmelt from the southern Rockies, which has sustained our farming and ranching operation for over 40 years, has diminished due to the gradually withering snow pack. Our streams used to run deep with run-off for months this time of year, but they are just trickles now. It is heartbreaking.<br>
<br>
      When I was a kid, we could produce 15,000 bales of grass and alfalfa hay a year. For the last five years, we have averaged just 1,500 bales. Our ranch used to support 250 mother cows, but they all had to go because the lack of water made our grasses increasingly unproductive and incapable of supporting that many cattle.<br>
<br>
    Incidences of disease that correlate with the changing weather have been another crippling blow to the livestock. Cases of Pigeon Fever spike in dry weather, and cases of the vesicular stomatitis virus increase in warm weather. Those have hit our horses and cattle hard during these extraordinarily brutal summers. During one of the severe dry spells last summer, we had a herd of 130 elk die mysteriously.<br>
<br>
      I wonder if my family farm would have survived for 42 years if today's conditions had existed back then. Ranching has always been a tough job, but these new conditions have made it even more difficult. Ranching has never been a way to make a lot of money even in the best-case scenario, but now it is simply unsustainable.<br>
<br>
         I'm sharing my story to bring awareness to the consequences of climate change in hopes of inspiring people to take action. <a href="http://ofa.bo/d0sN">Share your story</a> of how climate change is affecting your community.<br>
