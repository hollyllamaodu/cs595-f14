iPhone 'Bendgate' Is Apple's Mea Culpa

   [51]Comment Now
   [52]Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

   I like the iPhone 6, [53]a lot. My fellow contributor Ewan likes the
   iPhone 6 Plus, [54]a lot. Both models have flaws but they also set new
   benchmarks in smartphone design which rivals need to match. Or at least
   they did until [55]Apple [56]Apple shot itself in the foot.


   #Bendgate is the predictable term that has flooded social media over
   the last 24 hours. It comes after a video from [57]Unbox Therapy
   (below) went viral after demonstrating that it was relatively easy to
   bend the iPhone 6 Plus. The video itself was a response to scattered
   reports of the iPhone 6 Plus bending in owners’ front and back pockets.


   IFRAME: [58]http://www.youtube.com/embed/znK652H6yQM


   This started a flame war between Apple fans and haters and a number of
   defences were written including ‘[59]Duh: Of Course the iPhone 6 Plus
   Can Bend in Your Pocket’ on Wired. Unbox Therapy then poured petrol on
   these flames with a second video (below) showing not only that trying
   to straighten a bent iPhone 6 Plus can shatter the screen, but that
   under the same tests a Galaxy Note 3 was fine.


   IFRAME: [60]http://www.youtube.com/embed/FwM4ypi3at0


   Suddenly the argument that ‘all phones bend, stupid’ was no longer a
   great defence. They may, but the iPhone 6 Plus bends more.


   Antennagate v2.0

   So where does the blame lie? From my perspective I don’t think it can
   be shifted from Apple.


   If the iPhone 6 Plus is vulnerable in a pocket it should contain a
   suitable warning to that effect. Expecting users to change age old
   habits without warning is impossible and, like ‘[61]Antennagate’, the
   whole situation seems to have arrived through a lack of real world
   testing.
   New iPhones are ultra thin (image credit: Gordon Kelly)

   New iPhones are ultra thin (image credit: Gordon Kelly)


   Yet more to the point Bendgate also exists because of Apple being
   Apple. I’ve yet to have a reader tell me the iPhone 5S was too thick
   (feel free to do so!), but I’ve seen many complain about battery life.


   Of course the new iPhones do have improved battery life, but they
   could’ve revolutionised smartphone and phablet usage had Apple made
   them just thick enough to fit a flush camera – the lack of which Apple
   has been keen to [62]hide in promotional photos. Two problems: one
   stone.


   But Apple didn’t because more than a decade of unprecedented success
   has made the company confident it knows what its users want better than
   they know themselves. In many cases Apple has been proved right, until
   now. After all pride often comes before a fall.

   [63]Read more – iPhone 6: Why Apple Made Massive New iPhones


   iPhone 6 protruding camera (image credit: Gordon Kelly)

   iPhone 6 protruding camera (image credit: Gordon Kelly)


   Wider Industry Fallout

   There is an inevitable (and in many ways understandable) reaction: mock
   Apple. Go ahead, enjoy it – needlessly skinny phone, bulbous camera,
   snaps like a twig – but owners of rival handsets should know this could
   be every bit as damaging for them in the long run as for iPhone owners.


   Why? Because like it or not Apple remains the single biggest driving
   force in the industry. I’m an Android user with no inclination to
   switch, but I recognise [64]Google [65]Google and [66]Microsoft
   [67]Microsoft’s long adoption of NFC will only gain real world traction
   now Apple is aboard.


   For me Android has surpassed iOS and ‘Android L’ has me drooling over
   videos on [68]Material Design, but there is no disputing the early
   influence of iOS and had the original iPhone not broken new ground (the
   first [69]Android phone had a keyboard) I suspect Google’s OS would be
   a very different and less impressive thing today.

   HTC One M8 (image credit: Gordon Kelly)

   HTC One M8 (image credit: Gordon Kelly)


   Apple’s industry influence brings out the best in its rivals – whether
   playing catch up or inspiring them to leap ahead, but where the
   majority of Android phones are still behind iPhones ([70]HTC One aside
   – pictured above) is design. Or they were until the tacky design of the
   otherwise brilliant Note range was shown to be structurally superior to
   the latest and greatest iPhones.

   Consequently I’m concerned. I don’t want Apple rivals to feel venerated
   when they should be using the iPhone 6 and iPhone 6 Plus as the
   springboard to new and better things. Apple just showed the world metal
   chassis smartphones don’t need to be heavy, now go prove they don’t
   need to bend as well!

   It is an exciting time for Apple rivals, if they handle it the right
   way (that means [71]with dignity Samsung). Combined with [72]iOS 8
   upgrade problems, Apple suddenly has its hands full placating angry
   iPhone owners, addressing warranty questions and convincing hesitant
   upgraders to still make the jump.

   The opportunity for rivals to evolve is there if they approach it
   correctly. The ‘iFanny Pack’ will not be an easy sell.


   Update: Unbox Therapy has posted a third video testing the HTC One M8,
   Motorola Moto X (2014 model), Nokia 1020 and iPhone 5S. None of the
   phones bent in any significant fashion:

   IFRAME: [73]http://www.youtube.com/embed/IROcoJeVfSI#t=30
