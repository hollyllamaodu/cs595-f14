		<h1 itemprop="headline">Is Ebola Coming To America? Experts Ponder As Ebola Epidemic Worsens In Africa.</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

	<div class="comment_links">
		<a href="#comment_reply" class="scroll_to post_your_comment">Comment Now</a>
		<div class="comments_follow followable_block">




    <div class="followable_block initializing" data-target-type="article" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/2238-3241" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Is Ebola Coming To America? Experts Ponder As Ebola Epidemic Worsens In Africa.&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-type-advoice="false" data-target-name="" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow">

			<div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">

				<div class="body">

					<div class="body_inner">



						
<p><i>Sept. 30 update: CDC <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/dandiamond/2014/09/30/ebola-comes-to-america-cdc-confirms-first-case-of-ebola-diagnosed-in-united-states/">has confirmed</a> the first case of Ebola diagnosed in the United States.</i></p> 
<p>New CDC and WHO data suggest that Africa’s Ebola outbreak is worse than expected —&nbsp;and in a worst-case scenario, the current Ebola epidemic could become endemic.</p> 
<p>And those gloomy projections have experts worried that Ebola will inevitably arrive in the United States.</p> 
<p><a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/dandiamond/2014/09/30/ebola-comes-to-america-cdc-confirms-first-case-of-ebola-diagnosed-in-united-states/"><strong>Sept. 30 Update: First Case Of Ebola Diagnosed in United States</strong></a></p> 
<p>According to CDC <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/su63e0923a1.htm?s_cid=su63e0923a1_e">modeling</a> released on Tuesday, Liberia and Sierra Leone are on track to have 21,000 cases of Ebola by September 30, unless the situation rapidly improves. (The CDC’s estimate is based on an assumption that <a href="https://twitter.com/ddiamond/status/514438123338874880">more than half</a> of all Ebola cases will not be reported or detected.)</p> 
<p>CDC also estimates that as many as 1.4 million people in Liberia and Sierra Leone could contract Ebola by January 20.</p> 
<ul> 
 <li>Given that about 10 million people live in Liberia and Sierra Leone, that estimate suggests that about one in seven residents would contract Ebola.</li> 
 <li>The current outbreak has a mortality rate of more than 70%.</li> 
</ul> 
<p>(For a sense of perspective on CDC’s worst-case scenario for West Africa: If a similarly sized outbreak happened in the United States, more than 45 million Americans would contract Ebola.)</p> 
<p><a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/dandiamond/2014/08/22/americans-stop-worrying-about-ebola-every-suspected-case-in-u-s-has-been-a-false-alarm/"><strong>Also See: Nearly 40% Of Americans Fear An Ebola Outbreak</strong></a></p> 
<p>In separate projections released on Monday, the World Health Organization offered up a dire warning: There might not be an easy solution for the current Ebola epidemic, given the outbreak’s rate and pace.</p> 
<p>Instead, Ebola could become a lasting problem for Africa.</p> 
<p>“We must therefore face the possibility that [Ebola] will become endemic among the human population of West Africa, a prospect that has never previously been contemplated,” the WHO Ebola Response Team wrote in the <em><a href="http://www.nejm.org/doi/pdf/10.1056/NEJMoa1411100">New England Journal of Medicine</a></em>.</p> 
<p>“The risk of continued epidemic expansion and the prospect of endemic EVD in West Africa call for the most forceful implementation of present control measures and for the rapid development and deployment of new drugs and vaccines.”</p> 
<p>However, in a best-case scenario, CDC says that the new mobilization of public health efforts and U.S. military support will be able to contain Ebola and end the current epidemic by January 20.</p> 
<p>“My gut feeling is, the actions we’re taking now are going to make that worst-case scenario not come to pass,” Dr. Thomas R. Frieden, who runs the CDC, told the <em><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/09/24/health/ebola-cases-could-reach-14-million-in-4-months-cdc-estimates.html">New York Times</a></em>. “But it’s important to understand that it could happen.”</p> 
<p><strong>Bad News In Africa Only Bodes Poorly For World<br /> </strong>The continued persistence of the Ebola epidemic has experts increasingly&nbsp;pessimistic about a quick solution.</p> 
<p>Ebola cases in West Africa are rising faster than hospital beds are available. And the outbreak’s spread is seemingly accelerating. (Chart via virologist <a href="https://twitter.com/MackayIM/status/514274999624941568">Ian Mackay</a>.)</p> 
<p><img class=" size-full wp-image-3244" alt="Ebola" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/dandiamond/files/2014/09/Ebola.png" /></p> 
<p>The longer the Ebola outbreak rages, the more likely it is that the disease will mutate into an even more dangerous form. Scott Gottlieb, who served as a top FDA official under President George W. Bush, believes that at least several cases of Ebola will come to America, and has <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/scottgottlieb/2014/09/03/can-ebola-go-airborne/">warned</a> that there’s even a chance that the disease could go airborne.</p> 
<p>But there are signs of hope, even in West Africa. As David Kroll —&nbsp;who has so ably tracked the Ebola outbreak for <em>Forbes</em> —&nbsp;<a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/davidkroll/2014/09/23/nigeria-free-of-ebola-as-final-surveillance-contacts-are-released/">reported</a> yesterday, nearby Nigeria has been able to remain free from Ebola. At Vox, Brad Plumer has <a href="http://www.vox.com/2014/9/19/6543157/ebola-is-unlikely-to-go-airborne">reviewed</a> the evidence of Ebola going airborne, and finds that the odds are “very unlikely.”</p> 
<p>Government officials also continue to stress that there’s little chance of a mass Ebola outbreak in the United States, a point that the NIH’s Anthony Fauci <a href="http://foreignaffairs.house.gov/hearing/subcommittee-hearing-global-efforts-fight-ebola">reiterated</a>&nbsp;to Congress last week. And even if a few Ebola patients arrive in the U.S., the nation’s hospitals would be well-equipped to handle them.</p> 
<p><a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/dandiamond/2014/08/22/americans-stop-worrying-about-ebola-every-suspected-case-in-u-s-has-been-a-false-alarm/"><strong>Also See: All 68 Suspected Cases Of Ebola In United States Have Been False Alarms</strong></a></p> 
<p>“Controlling Ebola is not a very sophisticated task,” Dr. Amesh Adalja, a biosecurity specialist at the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center, told <em><a href="http://thehill.com/homenews/senate/217969-congress-worries-ebola-could-hit-us">The Hill</a></em> last week. “Ebola outbreaks are stopped in their tracks when basic public health measures are in place … and the United States would not be a hospitable environment for something that spreads exclusively through blood and body fluids.”</p> 
<p>Writing in the Advisory Board Daily Briefing, we’ve tracked the Ebola epidemic’s spread pretty closely, from covering the <a href="http://www.advisory.com/Daily-Briefing/2014/04/02/Guinea-struggles-with-unprecedented-outbreak-of-Ebola">early outbreak</a> in Guinea to creating a <a href="http://www.advisory.com/daily-briefing/blog/2014/08/ebola-how-the-deadliest-outbreak-in-history-spread">timeline</a> of how the deadliest Ebola epidemic in history has spread.</p> 
<p>Earlier this month, researchers <a href="http://www.advisory.com/daily-briefing/2014/09/15/what-are-the-odds-that-ebola-makes-it-to-america">estimated</a> that there was up to an 18% chance that at least one case of Ebola would arrive in the United States by September 22. (Not counting the four health workers intentionally brought to the U.S. for treatment.)<br /> 
 <!--nextpage--><br /> As of press time on September 23, the number of Ebola cases in the United States remained at zero.</p>

<p>It seems that when it comes to the fear of a potential outbreak of Ebola in the U.S., the conversation overwhelmingly centers around a possible mutation that would allow the virus to go airborne, thus making any potential outbreak extremely difficult to control. Without such a mutation, more often than not, authors point out the superiority of the U.S. health care system and resources which would “no doubt” limit any potential outbreak. The controlled response in Nigeria and Senegal are referred to often as well, as an illustration of what would likely happen if the virus was to spread to more developed international countries. There are a number of factors that people must begin to consider:</p> 
<p>1) In Senegal there was only one suspected case, yet 74 separate contacts had to be found, (immediately no less), and tracked for more than 3 weeks to ensure the spread was eliminated. In Nigeria, there were 21 cases, all of whom had to be interviewed at length, after which, per the WHO’s Situation Report, 874 contacts had to be located and monitored for more than three weeks, many of whom are still being monitored. The human resources required to pull this off were enormous. The reported and unreported cases are now well over 6,000 in Liberia, Guinea and Sierra Leone, so you can imagination how difficult it has now become to get ahead of the spread, especially considering so many of the infected have not been interviewed and potential contacts are not being monitored, (not to mention that in urban centers, it is extremely difficult to truly discover all potential contacts). As such, it is not remotely unrealistic to expect, even with the additional resources being sent into these countries, that the outbreak will reach into the hundreds of thousands by early in the new year.</p> 
<p>2) Given the exponential growth of Ebola, (doubling every 3 weeks), it has been reported, and is statistically clear anyway, that the R factor is 2, meaning every infected person infects another 2 people. With this type of infectiousness, it is difficult to comprehend why people continue to believe that Ebola is only being spread through direct contact with infected, most notably the mishandling of the dead. A study conducted by the Canadian Food Inspection Agency and the Canadian Department of Health, published in Scientific Reports in 2012, put forth a very compelling argument that Ebola was transmitted through bodily fluids that could be propelled through the air and live for short periods of time, such as those fluids excreted through the act of sneezing or coughing. They had infected pigs in a cage within very close proximity to a cage of healthy non-human primates (NHP). The NHP could not directly touch the infected pigs, yet within a short period of time, many of the NHP had become infected. While I would never argue that Ebola is anything like the flu in this regard, it is not NEARLY as difficult to become infected as many authors and experts make on.<br /> 3) Based on the number of cases to come, the relative ease with which a person can become infected and the resources required to slow or stop a spread using traditional monitoring methods, I cannot fathom how people convince themselves that the West Africa outbreak will not spread internationally. With each new case, it becomes more and more difficult to confine the spread to the three countries currently most impacted. It will eventually make its way out, as it has already, but it will do so in a much more aggressive way, making it very difficult to control. When the first cases were discovered in Nigeria, the total number of infected was only 1,000, making it much easier to allocate resources. Try allocating appropriate resources when the number of cases is in the hundreds of thousands, or millions.</p> 
<p>If/when this comes to the U.S., I fully expect multiple clusters and eventually a breakout difficult to control. But here’s hoping I’m wrong.</p>
