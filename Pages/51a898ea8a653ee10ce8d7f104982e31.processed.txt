Malcolm's Moment: Inside Forbes' Secret Hip-Hop Heritage

   [69]Comment Now
   [70]Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

   IFRAME: [71]http://www.youtube.com/embed/v1iDNcGcfu8

   Today marks the launch of the eighth annual [72]Hip-Hop Cash Kings
   list, which measures the top earners in the genre. But here at FORBES,
   we’ve had our eye on rap much longer than many people
   realize—including, until recently, yours truly.

   To be sure, hip-hop acts graced this publication’s pages a few times
   prior to 2007. There was time 50 Cent landed in the pages of the Celeb
   100 issue in 2006 and an interview with Jay Z in 2003; in 1999, Diddy
   appeared on the cover alongside [73]Jerry Seinfeld.

   Apparently the lineage goes even deeper, according to Fab 5 Freddy, who
   told me quite a story between bites of pizza at Roberta’s in Brooklyn
   not too long ago. Back in 1989, the Grammys added the category Best Rap
   Performance to accommodate the growing genre. Nominees that first year
   included DJ Jazzy Jeff & The Fresh Prince, LL Cool Jay and
   Salt-N-Peppa.

   Due to time restrictions, however, the presentation of the award was
   relegated to the pre-telecast portion of the Grammys—in other words,
   tossed into the vast anonymous pool inhabited by the most critically
   acclaimed denizens of niche genres like Zydeco and Hawaiian music. The
   forefathers of today’s Hip-Hop Cash Kings were less than pleased.

   “We decided to throw a boycott-the-Grammys-party Grammy night in L.A.,”
   recalls Fab, who had just started hosting Yo! [74]MTV [75]MTV Raps less
   than a year earlier. “Turned out to be the hottest best-attended party
   that year.”

   Guests included Ice T and Slick Rick, who did a television spot with
   Fab for his then-brand-new show, as well as [76]Will Smith, DJ Jazzy
   Jeff and Dennis Hopper. But the arrival who called the biggest stir was
   the man who drove up on a motorcycle: none other than Malcolm Forbes.

   Fab and his compatriots were flabbergasted. Unsure of what to say, one
   of them asked him how he felt about rap.

   “Hey man,” he said confidently, “I’ve got the chapter and the verse!”

   Fab’s uncertainty quickly turned to elation.

   “That kind of love and support was an indication of how broad based
   this culture really was,” he recalls. “American got to see that Malcolm
   Forbes from [77]Forbes Magazine was down with the program.”

   For the full story, check out the YouTube video above.

   For more on the business of music, check out my book [78]Michael
   Jackson, Inc and my Jay Z biography [79]Empire State of Mind. You can
   follow me on [80]Twitter & [81]Facebook.
