		<h1 itemprop="headline">Where Have All The Grownups Gone? Why 'Adulthood' As We Know It Is Dead</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

	<div class="comment_links">
		<a href="#comment_reply" class="scroll_to post_your_comment">Comment Now</a>
		<div class="comments_follow followable_block">




    <div class="followable_block initializing" data-target-type="article" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/1462-9116" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Where Have All The Grownups Gone? 			<div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">

				<div class="body">

					<div class="body_inner">



						
<p><span class="tweet_quote"> <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1r09fQh&amp;text=Would%20you%20rather%20buy%20Super%20Bowl%20tickets%20or%20a%20Vitamix%20blender%3F%20Your%20answer%20may%20depend%20on%20your%20age">Would you rather buy Super Bowl tickets or a Vitamix blender? Your answer may depend on your age<span></span></a></span> . Recent survey research from Eventbrite <a href="http://www.foxbusiness.com/personal-finance/2014/09/17/eventbrite-millennials-driven-by-fomo-spending-more-on-experience-economy/" target="_blank">found</a> that <span class="tweet_quote"> <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1r09fQh&amp;text=Millenials%20are%20increasing%20prioritizing%20spending%20on%20events%20and%20ephemeral%20experiences%20over%20tangible%20assets">Millenials are increasingly prioritizing spending on events and ephemeral experiences over tangible assets<span></span></a></span> . Is this refusal to delay gratification yet another sign of a generation that refuses to grow up?</p> 
<p>We have twentysomethings living at home, <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2014/01/20/does-marriage-make-sense-for-millennials/">holding off on marriage</a> and families, <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2014/05/28/the-dirty-30s-over-40-of-oldest-millennials-are-still-financially-dependent-on-mom-and-dad/">relying on their parents</a> for financial handouts and <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2012/02/09/an-open-letter-to-helicopter-parents-and-their-offspring/">job interview support</a>, <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2014/06/23/why-taking-a-job-you-hate-right-out-of-college-is-the-best-career-move-you-can-make/">eschewing unfulfilling jobs</a>, favoring public transit over cars and <a href="http://brooklyn.ny1.com/content/lifestyles/money_matters/215676/survey--nearly-two-thirds-of-millennials-do-not-have-a-credit-card/">not showing much interest</a> in building up their credit scores. In short, they’re shrugging off many of the markers that have traditionally defined American adulthood. &nbsp;In some cases, Millennial lifestyle choices are constrained by monetary realities (student loan repayments vs. a new Subaru is no contest), but, more broadly, there’s an attitudinal sea change evident here. After all, the idea of a “dream job” <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2014/07/22/james-garner-elaine-stritch-kanye-west-and-the-culture-of-creative-cowardice/">would seem downright ludicrous</a> to our grandparents.</p> 
<p>&nbsp; </p> 
<div class="zemanta-img"> 
 <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 680px">
  <img class="zemanta-img-configured " title="English: Doesn't this playground look like so ..." alt="English: Doesn't this playground look like so ..." src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/jmaureenhenderson/files/2014/09/670px-playground_at_fuji-hakone-izu_national_park.jpg" />
  <p class="wp-caption-text">English: Doesn’t this playground look like so much fun? (Photo credit: Wikipedia)</p>
 </div> 
</div> 
<p>This disinclination to grow up is reinforced by popular culture and technology. We devour YA novels and superhero movies. We have an entire <a href="http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2014/05/01/inside-the-bizarre-world-of-bronies-adult-male-fans-of-my-little-pony.html">subculture of grown men</a> who are ardent fans of cartoon ponies meant for little girls. We eagerly buy tickets to see boy band reunion tours where the “boys” are now mostly married dads pushing 40, who in decades past, would be relegated to playing state fairs and casinos. In our nostalgia-driven culture, they still have currency. We <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/adult-summer-camp-club-getaway-2014-8?op=1">can even pay big bucks</a> to regress back to the days when we penned Mom and Dad letters from Camp Sleepy Pines, except in this new iteration, <em>craft time</em> refers to craft beer and campers are as likely to arrive with condoms as they are bug spray. Welcome to a world in which all you ever need outgrow is clothing.</p> 
<p>The mores of high school persist into adulthood via our engagement with social media. The <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2012/07/11/is-social-media-destroying-your-self-esteem/">self esteem effects</a> of comparing ourselves to others on Facebook or Instagram is as real as making those same <em>“Is her life better than mine?”</em> comparisons in homeroom. Online, we flout the norms of hierarchy and formality. <a href="http://www.psmag.com/navigation/books-and-culture/um-professors-creeping-facebook-page-90906/">Your professor friends you on Facebook</a>. You can follow your boss’s Twitter. We’re all 15 again. Taken to a deceitful and sad extreme, we end up with a <a href="http://www.buzzfeed.com/katiejmbaker/why-did-charity-johnson-pretend-to-be-a-teenager-for-20-year">story</a> like that of Charity Johnson, who has spent her adult life to date pretending to be a teen and using Facebook to seek out a string of mother figures to rescue her.</p> 
<p>A. O. Scott <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/09/14/magazine/the-death-of-adulthood-in-american-culture.html?ref=todayspaper">recently wrote</a> of the death of adulthood in popular culture and how we’re seeing entertainment that glorifies permanent adolescence. Patriarchs of film and television have been replaced by man children and women whose first visions of aspirational adulthood came from <em>Sex And The City</em>.</p> 
<p>“I do feel the loss of something here, but bemoaning the general immaturity of contemporary culture would be as obtuse as declaring it the coolest thing ever. A crisis of authority is not for the faint of heart. It can be scary and weird and ambiguous. But it can be a lot of fun, too. The best and most authentic cultural products of our time manage to be all of those things. They imagine a world where no one is in charge and no one necessarily knows what’s going on, where identities are in perpetual flux. Mothers and fathers act like teenagers; little children are wise beyond their years. Girls light out for the territory and boys cloister themselves in secret gardens. We have more stories, pictures and arguments than we know what to do with, and each one of them presses on our attention with a claim of uniqueness, a demand to be recognized as special. The world is our playground, without a dad or a mom in sight,” he writes.</p> 
<p>While Scott offers mixed praise for a Peter Pan effect in entertainment and Laurence Steinberg <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/09/21/opinion/sunday/the-case-for-delayed-adulthood.html?ref=todayspaper">muses</a> that delaying adulthood could mean increased brain plasticity, what is the downside of living in a world where we’re no longer required to put away childish things? Certainly, it’s a trend that has the auto and housing industries on edge, but what about the effects of arrested development on the individual? <span class="tweet_quote"> <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1r09fQh&amp;text=How%20healthy%20is%20it%20for%2030%20year-olds%20to%20be%20going%20to%20summer%20camp%3F">How healthy is it for 30 year-olds to be going to summer camp?<span></span></a></span></p> 
<p>For author <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jmaureenhenderson/2014/06/18/the-sad-secret-successful-people-dont-want-you-to-know/">Anneli Rufus</a>, a retreat to the past is less about embracing a mindset that rejects traditional maturation into rigid roles and more about being mired in fear of the present or future and unable to catch up to either. Protracted adolescence is an anxious state in which to live. As she writes in <em>Stuck: Why We Can’t (or Won’t) Move On</em>:</p> 
<p>“We are stuck in the past, not usually by choice, but because, like dud popcorn kernels or bonsai trees, we failed to grow. The ones who were supposed to show us how did not. They did not know or were not there. Portals inscribed with mystical initiation signs gleamed in the moonlight through which we were meant to walk, but no: We wandered back the other way or balked. Or something happened way back when and bang. The end.”</p> 
<p>She’s not writing exclusively about young adults, but she very well could be.</p> 
<p>Despite hand-wringing to the contrary, today’s youth <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/08/03/health/03mind.html">aren’t significantly</a> more narcissist than previous generations, but the time horizon during which they are free to focus on themselves <em>does</em> extend far longer. Without mortgage payments or midnight feedings, they’re able to devote their attention and income to self-improvement and self indulgence almost exclusively, which is why Eventbrite’s findings aren’t particularly surprising. Instead of the traditional secular Rumspringa-like interregnum between being parented and being a parent that coincided with the college years, that period of reduced responsibility has been stretching into the late 20s and early 30s. And we’re largely okay with that. Suddenly, <i>Girls</i>’ Hannah all but demanding her parents continue to support her post-college literary ambitions seems gauche but unsurprising and <em>13 Going On 30</em> feels more like a documentary than a cute romcom.</p> 
<p>It’s worth asking ourselves when time finally comes for Millennials to assume the mantle of adulthood, whether by biological imperative or economic necessity (Boomer parents are not immortal), how ready will they really be?</p>
