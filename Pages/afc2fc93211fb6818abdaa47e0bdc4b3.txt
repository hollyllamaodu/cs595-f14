		<h1 itemprop="headline">6 Concrete Steps To Building A Collaborative Culture That Inspires</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

				<div class="body">

					<div class="body_inner">



						
<p>This summer, I had the pleasure of visiting the headquarters of <a href="http://www.execunet.com/execunetcms/leadership/" target="_blank">ExecuNet</a>, an online membership community 300,000+ members strong, dedicated to supporting and enriching executives around the globe.&nbsp; &nbsp;The minute I stepped through the door, I could “feel” the culture in a palpable way. It was inviting, upbeat, open, warm, and casual.&nbsp; People just seemed happier and more relaxed than what I’ve observed in many corporate offices in which I’ve worked, coached and trained.</p> 
<p>Then something amazing happened.&nbsp; The VP of Finance, Maggie&nbsp;Pisani (whom I’d never met and didn’t know by name), walked up to me as I was sitting in chair waiting for my meeting, and said, “Hi Kathy! I’ve heard a lot about you, and we’re all excited to have you today. I’m so sorry I can’t join you – I’m racing out to a baseball game. Please excuse my very casual attire! But I look forward to hearing all about your meeting and seeing you again soon.” Others welcomed me openly too, and our meeting revealed the same happy, collaborative vibe.</p> 
<p>Immediately, I knew this work culture was special, and after the meeting, my feeling was confirmed.&nbsp; I asked Tony Vlahos, Chief Marketing Officer and Mark Anderson, President of ExecuNet, to share with me more about the culture, and some lessons other leaders and small businesses can learn to help them build open, collaborative (and happy) workplaces.</p> 
<p><img class=" size-full wp-image-4568" alt="At meeting" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/kathycaprino/files/2014/09/iStock_000025631892Small.jpg" /><b><br /> Here’s what they shared:</b></p> 
<p><b>Kathy Caprino:</b> Tony, what goes into making a collaborative culture that encourages loyalty, trust and cooperation, exactly?</p> 
<p><b>Tony Vlahos:</b> We believe that a collaborative culture is based on openness, complete transparency and building trust.&nbsp; It also focuses its energy on the search for solutions to challenges and leveraging opportunities rather than on placing blame or searching for “guilty.”</p> 
<p>Here, we are vigilantly committed to being open and transparent with our team and employees. At monthly staff meetings, we keep the entire team aware of how we are doing—the “good, bad and the “ugly” — in terms of our customer feedback and performance.&nbsp; We also regularly have middle managers present and showcase themselves and their teams to encourage real ownership and a focus on results at all levels.</p> 
<p>You’ve heard of Tonys and Emmys. Well, our award here is a brand statement we call “MELBAs” – Making Executives Lives Better.&nbsp; We’ve created these awards and utilize them regularly to recognize the contributions of employees who’ve made a difference. Employees proudly hang these recognition awards in their cubicles and offices.</p> 
<p>Since our corporate assets walk out the door each night, over the years, we’ve also made it clear that people are what’s important — and not just what they do in business.&nbsp; Advocacy, flexibility, empathy and support for our employees who go through tough times and in their personal lives is something that sets apart a collaborative, caring organization.&nbsp; A respect for family commitments outside the office enables team members to avoid missing that special event.&nbsp; When people leave, we traditionally continue to view them as an extended ExecuNet “family”— such that some have been rehired at a later date—and continue to maintain strong ties.</p> 
<p><b>Kathy:</b> Do you think a collaborative, thriving culture can be built and sustained if the top leaders don’t have the right mindset?</p> 
<p><strong>Tony:</strong> No.&nbsp; It’s not possible.&nbsp; Staff looks to leadership to set the tone — always has and always will.&nbsp; If the passion, commitment and follow-through are missing, the workforce knows it.&nbsp; If the top leaders don’t consistently show respect for the people they work with and treat people fairly and well, they won’t elicit the commitment and collaboration they expect or want.</p> 
<p>We also believe in keeping the sense of hierarchy as low as possible so that everyone feels they can be equal contributors.&nbsp; All offices are roughly the same size with the same size large internal windows.&nbsp; This limits the feeling of “closed door meetings” and levels the playing field, reducing the experience of silos.</p> 
<p>Here, the Chairman, David Opton, and the President have always exemplified the type of servant leadership that helps galvanize the team.&nbsp; The small things make a big difference. From being the last to get new furniture, to participating in parking in the most distant parking places when it was their turn — they put into practice what is expected of leadership and the entire workforce.</p> 
<p>Keeping things light with humor is also an important part of the culture and mindset.&nbsp; Having fun while you work is an ingredient we like to reinforce and keep in balance.&nbsp; It sets a positive, fun tone for the organization.</p> 
<p><b>Kathy:</b> What are a few small steps that ExecuNet takes, and others can adopt easily, to keep their cultures thriving?<br /> 
 <!--nextpage--><br /> <b>Tony:</b> We do a number of unusual things that are highly effective:</p> 
<p><b>Communicate openly every day to the entire staff</b></p> 
<p>Everyday team members get a short internal email, essentially a blog post, called the <i>ExecuNet Buzz</i>.&nbsp; It highlights the “goings on for the day”–&nbsp; visitors, major meetings, ins and outs of staff, birthdays, anniversaries, etc.</p> 
<p>Its purpose is to keep things transparent and reduce the “water cooler” talk about what is going on or who’s visiting and walking around.&nbsp; It includes a Trivia Question for the day and inspiring and humorous quotes to inform, enlighten and engage the team.&nbsp;&nbsp; Those who answer the Trivia Question incorrectly get humorously highlighted in the next day’s Buzz but employees start their day with a fun read.</p> 
<p><b>Involve the entire team in interviewing and onboarding of new hires </b></p> 
<p>We conduct thorough interviewing and on-boarding of new hires as a team approach, so candidates get a very good feel for collaboration and teamwork from the outset – and also get a taste of the intimacy of meeting and working with those they’ll be collaborating with in the future.</p> 
<p>We’ve also implemented an extensive on-boarding approach for new hires so they meet personally with each department for lunch in the first several weeks without a business purpose.&nbsp; It breaks down barriers around who the “newbie” is (regardless of rank) from the outset.</p> 
<p><b>Reinforce your history</b></p> 
<p>We maintain traditions and keep alive our ExecuNet history through pictures and funny stories to capture and support our culture.&nbsp; Many stories surround our events.&nbsp; Since we encourage team members to dress in costume for Halloween to show their creativity and their “other selves,” we have lots to share.&nbsp; Prizes are voted on by the entire company.</p> 
<p><b>Celebrate personal life</b></p> 
<p>For example, we celebrate birthdays in reverse.&nbsp; Instead of team members celebrating another teammate’s birthday as a small group, the person with the birthday brings in things to celebrate their own birthday and they share this with the entire company—so no one feels left out.&nbsp; As we have grown larger, we’ve learned that you move quickly when “treats” are put out.</p> 
<p><b>Honor the individual</b></p> 
<p>We celebrate the individual at our monthly staff meeting in an activity called the “For the Good of the Order.” &nbsp;At the end of our monthly staff meetings, a quick go around of sharing is enabled so anyone can share something that is personally important to them – typically something not work related but sometimes a recognition of help from another person.&nbsp; It is not mandatory but comes with the express requirement that no one reacts to the sharing—just listens.&nbsp; It is meant as real leveler for people and gives them all a chance to share something important to them.</p> 
<p><b>Encourage personal connection </b></p> 
<p>We make our employees feel a part of the organization by encouraging them to get to know everyone on the team.&nbsp; We’ve established an independent employee group, known as “The Committee,” to plan team events, picnics, lunches, etc.&nbsp; We give them a small budget and every 6-8 weeks they plan interactions and experiences that help people get to know others.&nbsp; These events have ranged from an “international luncheon” where our diverse group of employees brings in a special dish to celebrate the opening of baseball season, to a jeopardy game about company history.&nbsp; Our “Why Wine” events at the end of the day are an excuse to celebrate a recent success or important event.</p> 
<p>In the end, we know that our culture and the way we interact and engage is the single most important thing we can build and create, and we focus on it every day.<br />
