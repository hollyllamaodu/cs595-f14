		<h1 itemprop="headline">How To Earn More From Your Cash</h1>

<p>Generating yield in the short-term market is challenging—but it isn’t impossible. You may be able to boost the income your cash allocation produces if you’re willing to take on a bit more risk.</p> 
<p>To determine whether it makes sense to accept greater risk in exchange for greater yield potential, consider the purpose for your cash investments. In some cases, you may require absolute stability. In others you may be willing to accept some possibility of small declines in exchange for higher interest payments. The decision comes down to your needs: the role this allocation plays in your overall portfolio, and what you hope to get out of it given your needs and time horizon.</p> 
<p>You may be able to wring more yield from your cash by taking on the following risks:</p> 
<p><strong>Credit risk</strong></p> 
<p>Corporate issues pay significantly more yield than Treasuries, to compensate for the greater risk that the issuer will default. The greater the risk, the larger the yield premium a corporate bond or bill offers compared with a Treasury with a comparable term. The spread—the gap between yields—of corporate bonds over Treasury bonds grew to historically wide margins during the 2008 financial crisis, and since then has come down considerably.</p> 
<p><img class=" size-full wp-image-1262" alt="earningmore_cash727x285" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/fidelity/files/2014/10/earningmore_cash727x285.jpg" /></p> 
<p>&nbsp;</p> 
<p><strong>Interest rate risk</strong></p> 
<p>Another way to secure higher yields is to hold securities with longer terms. In general, when two bonds with comparable credit quality have different maturity dates, the one with the longer term will typically pay more yield. But longer-term bonds carry greater interest rate risk: If the general level of interest rates rises, bond prices will usually fall—and they’ll fall further for longer-term bonds than for shorter-term bonds.</p> 
<p>A bond’s sensitivity to interest rate changes is expressed by its duration (technically, the weighted average time until the bond’s future payments). As a rule of thumb, an investment-grade security with duration of 1.9 could be expected to lose approximately 1.9% each time interest rates rise by 1%. Likewise, it could gain 1.9% when interest rates drop by 1%.</p> 
<p>Considering that interest rates are hovering at or near their historic lows, they have much more room to go up than down, causing many investors to worry about potential interest rate risk. Some investors are limiting their holdings to very short-term securities to limit the damage from any interest rate hikes. But these investors are earning little to no yield on such securities—and no one knows when interest rates will rise.</p> 
<p>According to Kim Miller, manager of <a title="Fidelity� Conservative Income Bond Fund" href="http://www.fidelity.com/conservativeincome" rel="nofollow">Fidelity<sup>&reg;</sup> Conservative Income Bond Fund</a> (FCONX), “An investor in a money market fund is probably earning between 0.01% and 0.10% right now. An investor willing to take on a little more price risk and principal volatility—less than a typical short- to intermediate-term bond fund might offer—could pick up between 0.20% and 0.30% in yield for some of their short-term assets by moving to a duration of three to six months.”</p> 
<p>In that case, you might consider shifting part of your short-term allocation to an ultra-short- or short-term bond fund. Or, if you prefer to invest through individual securities, you may want to build a ladder of bonds or CDs with sequential maturity dates—for example, holding equal amounts in securities with 6-, 12-, 24-, and 36-month maturities.</p> 
<p>“A six-month Treasury bill recently offered a 0.05% yield, a two-year Treasury note yielded 0.95%, and the three-year Treasury note as much as 0.95%,<sup>2</sup>”says Richard Carter, Fidelity vice president of fixed income products and services. “That is a significant differential. Providing that you are comfortable with the staging of your maturities, have enough cash on hand to meet your needs, and invest in high quality credits, you can allow the investments to mature at par (the face value of a bond), and not concern yourself with day-to-day price and valuation changes.”</p> 
<p><strong>&nbsp;Making your choice</strong></p> 
<p>When deciding how to invest your cash, make liquidity—how quickly you need access to the money—a central consideration. In general, the more comfortable you are with risk and the less liquidity you need, the more yield you can afford to pursue.</p>

<p><strong>The bottom line</strong></p> 
<p>Even in low-rate environments, there are ways to increase the returns on short-term investments. But with investing, there is no free lunch. More yield generally requires assuming more risk. The key: make sure your choices match up with your goals and your personal tolerance for risk.</p>

<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Credit ratings are forward-looking opinions about credit risk. <p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Credit ratings can also speak to the credit quality of an individual debt issue, such as a corporate note, a municipal bond, or a mortgage-backed security, and the relative likelihood that the issue may default.<br /> ‘AAA’—Extremely strong capacity to meet financial commitments. <p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">In general, the bond market is volatile, and fixed income securities carry interest rate risk. (As interest rates rise, bond prices usually fall, and vice versa. This effect is usually more pronounced for longer-term securities.) Fixed income securities also carry inflation risk and credit and default risks for both issuers and counterparties. Unlike individual bonds, most bond funds do not have a maturity date, so holding them until maturity to avoid losses caused by price volatility is not possible. Lower-quality debt securities involve greater risk of default or price changes due to potential changes in the credit quality of the issuer.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">The municipal market can be affected by adverse tax, legislative, or political changes and the financial condition of the issuers of municipal securities.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Any fixed income security sold or redeemed prior to maturity may be subject to loss.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Changes in government regulations, changes in interest rates, and economic downturns can have a significant negative effect on issuers in the financial services sector.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">For the purposes of FDIC insurance coverage limits, all depository assets of the account holder at the institution that issued the CD will generally be counted toward the aggregate limit (usually $250,000) for each applicable category of account. FDIC insurance does not cover market losses. All of the new issue brokered CDs Fidelity offers are FDIC insured. For details on FDIC insurance limits, see www.fdic.gov.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Any fixed income security sold or redeemed prior to maturity may be subject to a substantial gain or loss. If sold prior to maturity, CDs may be sold on the secondary market subject to market conditions.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Floating-rate loans generally are subject to restrictions on resale and they sometimes trade infrequently in the secondary market, and as a result may be more difficult to value, buy, or sell. A floating-rate loan might not be fully collateralized, which may cause the floating-rate loan to decline significantly in value.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Increases in real interest rates can cause the price of inflation-protected debt securities to decrease.</p> 
<p style="font-family: arial; color: #888888; font-size: small; line-height: 16px;">Interest income generated by Treasury bonds and certain securities issued by U.S. territories, possessions, agencies, and instrumentalities is generally exempt from state income tax but is generally subject to federal income and alternative minimum taxes and may be subject to state alternative minimum taxes.</p>
