		<h1 itemprop="headline">How Parents Can Hedge Against Rising Tuition Prices</h1>



			<!-- Make individual group author appear -->

	<div class="comment_links">

					<div class="body_inner">



						
<p>Trying to figure out what tuition will cost 18 years from now when your newborn baby is ready for college can be as difficult as saving the money itself. So what can you do to guarantee the money you’ll save will actually help your child get through college? Invest in prepaid tuition plans.</p> 
<p><strong>What are prepaid tuition plans?&nbsp;</strong></p> 
<p>Prepaid tuition plans are a special kind of 529 plan, a tax-advantaged college investment plan where you purchase tomorrow’s tuition at today’s prices. There are two basic places you can purchase future tuition: from your state if they offer a prepaid option or from <a href="https://www.privatecollege529.com/OFI529/home.jsp?CMPID=529PPC2013&amp;gclid=CMH-_t6Q-sACFUMF7AodYxAAGA&amp;gclsrc=aw.ds">Private College 529 Plan</a>, which works with 275 private colleges. The state plans will generally offer guaranteed tuition options for state schools. However, there is generally an option for redeeming tuition units or credits at out-of-state and private schools. For instance, <a href="http://www.forbes.com/washington/">Washington</a>&nbsp;State would allow you to choose a private school and deduct the amount of the U<a href="http://www.forbes.com/colleges/university-of-washington-seattle-campus/">niversity of Washington’s</a> tuition and then you pay the difference. Some states such as Pennsylvania even have specific private college plans for their residents.</p> 
<p><strong>Who can participate?</strong></p> 
<p>To find out if your state offers a prepaid tuition plan, go to the <a href="http://www.collegesavings.org/viewState.aspx">collegesavings.org</a> website and select your state. You’ll see if there is a prepaid option in your state. If you’re state doesn’t offer one, don’t worry. You still may have options. States will often let you purchase their plan if a grandparent or divorced parent lives in that state. Check individual plan guidelines. Anyone, no matter what state they live in, qualifies for Private College 529 Plan.</p> 
<p><strong>How much do you need to invest?</strong></p> 
<p>It sounds great and all to purchase tomorrow’s tuition at today’s prices, but how many parents have a spare $10,000 in their bank accounts? Not many, but the good news is they don’t need it. Private College 529 Plan allows parents to buy in with as little as $500 over a 2-year period. That’s about $21 per month. But will that amount buy you much tuition? Not really, at least not if you never increase the amount you put in.</p> 
<p>However, let’s say you start off by just investing $25 per month for the first two years of a child’s life. Then, yay, no more diapers! You increase contributions by another $60 to $85 per month until the child is 5. Once your kindergartner no longer needs daycare, you throw in $200 per month of what you were spending on childcare to total a $285 monthly contribution. This plan, according to Nancy Farmer, president of Private College 529 Plan would help a student acquire two and two thirds years of prepaid tuition at their least expensive private college. At <a href="http://www.baylor.edu/">Baylor</a>, a student would have just over a year of prepaid tuition purchased.</p> 
<p>In comparison, in&nbsp;<a href="http://www.get.wa.gov/">Washington State</a> &nbsp;a parent could purchase 50 semester units for a newborn at $74 per month for 18 years. 50 semester units is half a year at the state’s most expensive college, which is currently the&nbsp;University of Washington. Then when the child is 5, parents could purchase another 150 semester units, a year and a half years at the state’s most expensive school, for $260 per month. The total tuition purchase would be 2 years, less tuition than what someone could buy &nbsp;for about the same money at the least expensive school in the Private College 529 Plan program. However, a parent could redeem the tuition at a regional college instead and get the same two and two thirds years of tuition covered for the least expensive school in the Private College 529 Plan.</p> 
<p>How do parents make up the difference in the total price of tuition for four years of college? Parents may send their children to community colleges for a year or two and then use prepaid tuition for the final years. Their child may also get scholarships or grants that make up the difference. Minimal student loan borrowing may also be the final funding source.</p> 
<p><b></b><strong>Bottomline&nbsp;</strong></p> 
<p>In the battle to pay for a child’s college education prepaid tuition plans are one potential resource families can use. However, paying for college for most parents takes strategic planning involving a variety of funding sources. Families should look at all their options and combinations of options.</p>

<p>The Private College 529 Plan is great but I use it in a different way. I took money I had saved in accounts with more growth than average tuition rate hikes. Once I got within 3 years of her starting college, I moved it to the Private College 529 Plan. I did this at that time for these reasons…<br /> 1.
